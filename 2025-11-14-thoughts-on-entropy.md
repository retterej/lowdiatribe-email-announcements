There's an innate drive in all life to rail against entropy — the natural tendency toward disorder, decay, and the eventual heat death of the universe. Our latest reflection explores this relentless pull toward disorder and what it means for leadership and personal growth.

I feel this pull constantly and have watched how systems naturally decay without conscious effort. Teams fragment. Communication breaks down. Shared understanding scatters like leaves in wind. It's not because people are lazy or don't care — it's because the universe itself tends toward randomness, and human systems are no exception.

I see this as the fundamental challenge to leadership: the conscious application of energy to create and maintain order in human systems. Not the rigid, authoritarian kind that crushes creativity, but the dynamic, adaptive kind that enables complex work to happen.

I will confess to being a surface abuser — any open space in my office accumulates loosely stacked piles of papers, books, and LEGO sets in various states of assembly. It's my "nature." The only way to fight the chaos is to adjust my tendencies through small, iterative changes rather than grand gestures.

"But here's what I find beautiful about this — the fight against disorder is what gives work meaning. When we create something valuable, we're participating in the universe's most fundamental creative act."
